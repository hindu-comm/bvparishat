> > I am attaching the above video for the perusal of all. The Maheshwar > Sutra have been misconstrued to have been composed for the sake of > abbreviations for the Ashtadhyayi. Patanjali in his first anhik has > given an example of a man who is offering water to his dead ancestors > sitting under a tree. This act of the man serves two purposes. The > ancestors are offered offerings as well as the tree is watered. In > the same way prescriptingthe Ashtadhyayi with the Maheshwar Sutras > serves two purposes. The pratyaharaswere useful in compiling the > Ashtadhyayi as they provided abbreviations. The other part was in > instructing the letters and that too in Sutra form. The same form in > which the Ashtadhyayi was compiled. A question arises naturally which > of the two was the main objective of the Maheshwar Sutras. In the > article 'A Method of Linguistic Description' Staal concludes 'The > analysis above may give a glimpse of the techniques used in Panini's > workshop. It also shows that a phonological analysis of the sounds of > a language, expressed in a mere list with subdivisions, can be fully > determined by the grammatical structure of the language'. Is this > true for all languages? The question is whether the nature of the > elements of a language (here letters or अक्षर) determine the nature of > the language or vice-a-versa. But Panini has not left it there. He > has arranged them into sutras which have been considered as Vedas by > Patanjali. The use of the indicatory mark is not casual. Panini has > used indicatory marks at many other places. >
>

> > What does the indicatory letter achieve? The indicatory letter helps > in completing the articulation of the last letter of the sequence.
> Thus the Maheshwar Sutras also help to instruct each of the letters in > the Sutras. This aspect of the Maheshwar Sutras seems to have been > completely ignored by the later commentators of the Ashtadhyayi except > Katyayana and Patanjali.

The latter has to appropriate the material in detail, to > analyse its different forms of development, to trace out their inner > connexion. Only after this work is done, can the actual movement be > adequately described. If this is done successfully, if the life of the > subject-matter is ideally reflected as in a mirror, then it may appear > as if we had before us a mere a priori construction'

To say that the > presentation of Sanskrit Grammar in the Ashtadhyayi was the method he > used for the investigation of the Sanskrit Language is misplaced.
