> >   Your comments remind me of an earlier time of these debates.
> When Lokmanya Tilak was a student at the Deccan College \[1870s\], he > had two influential teachers, Shridhar Ganesh Jinsiwale and Keropant > Chhatre. Jinsiwale was an instructor in Sanskrit, and argued that the > Vedas were in a form of language comprehensible to humans, and could > not have existed prior to the existence of the humans. He claimed > that the Vedas must be Pauruṣeya. Chhatre was professor of mathematics > and astronomy and had noticed references to specific positions of > Nakṣatras in the Veda. These two teachers inspired Tilak, who was > both a Sanskritist and a mathematician, to look for astronomical > indications in the Vedas to figure out the likely date of the > composition of the Vedas. This resulted in Tilak's several works on > the date of the Veda. Jinsiwale and Tilak were attacked severely by > the upholders of the traditional Apauruṣeyatva. \[It just so happens > that Shridhar Ganesh Jinsiwale was my great-great-grand-mother's > brother.\]
> > 
> > 
> >   And of course, the Apaureṣeyatva theory was not only criticized by > the Jains and Buddhists, it was also criticized by the > Nyāya-Vaiśeṣikas, albeit on different grounds. In modern times, there > is often an impression that the theory of Apaureṣeyatva was the only > unchallenged traditional Hindu view. The history is somewhat > different and varied. >
> 
> >
