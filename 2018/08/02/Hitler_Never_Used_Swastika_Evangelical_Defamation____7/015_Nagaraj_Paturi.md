The word*swastika*has appeared in English since the 1870s, replacing*gammadion*, from[Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")γαμμάδιον.^([\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-MigSym-12))It is alternatively spelled in contemporary texts as*svastika*,^([\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-18))while in the 19th- and early 20th-century, alternate spellings such as*suastika*were occasionally used.^([\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-19))It was derived from the[Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit")term ([Devanagari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devanagari "Devanagari"):[स्वस्तिक](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%B8%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%B5%E0%A4%B8%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%A4%E0%A4%BF%E0%A4%95 "wikt:स्वस्तिक")), which transliterates to**svastika**under the commonly used IAST transliteration system, but is pronounced closer to "swastika" when letters are used with their English values. The first attested use of the word swastika in a European text is found in 1871 with the publications of[Heinrich Schliemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Schliemann "Heinrich Schliemann"), who while crudely digging the[Hisarlik mound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hisarlik "Hisarlik")near the[Aegean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea")coast, for the lost history of Troy (Trojan war), discovered over 1,800 ancient samples of the swastika symbol and its variants. Schliemann linked his findings to the Sanskrit*swastika*.^([\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-boissoneaultsm-14)[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-20)[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-21))

The word*swastika*derives from the Sanskrit root*swasti*, which is composed of*su,*("good, well") and*asti*("it is, there is").^([\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-Schliemann347-22))The word*swasti*occurs frequently in the[Vedas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedas "Vedas")and it means "well, good, auspicious, luck, success, prosperity".^([\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-M-W-23)[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-24))Swastika is a derived word and connotes a form of welcome or a sign of something "associated with well-being".^([\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-M-W-23))According to[Monier-Williams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monier-Williams "Monier-Williams"), a majority of scholars consider it a solar symbol, and in the ancient Indian texts the base*swasti*is equivalent to "may it be well with thee! hail! health! adieu! so be it!".^([\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-M-W-23))The sign implies something fortunate, lucky or auspicious, and when applied to entrances, doors, mandalas or object it denotes or reminds of auspiciousness or well-being.^([\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-M-W-23))

The earliest known textual use of the word*swastika*is in[Panini's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini "Pāṇini")Ashtadhyayi, which uses it to explain one of the Sanskrit grammar rules, in the context of a type of identifying mark on a cow's ear.^([\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-Schliemann347-22))Most scholarship suggests Panini lived in or before mid 4th-century[BCE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Era "Common Era")(*[floruit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floruit "Floruit")*),^([\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-25)[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-26))possibly in 6th or 5th century BCE.^([\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-27)[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swastika#cite_note-28))
