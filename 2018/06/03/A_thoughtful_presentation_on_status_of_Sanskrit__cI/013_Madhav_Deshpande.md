After the conquest of Pune in 1818, the British opened a Pune Pathashala for Sanskrit and continued for some time the granting of annual Dakshina that was traditionally given by the Peshwas to learned Brahmins. The Pune Pathashala gradually morphed into the British funded Deccan College in 1860, where the teaching of Sanskrit continued along with the introduction of modern subjects. My great-great-grand-mother's brother Shridhar Ganesh Jinsiwale graduated with M.A. in Sanskrit from the Deccan College in 1873, and as a teaching fellow taught Lokamanya Tilak at this institution. Later he became professor of Sanskrit at the Wilson College in Mumbai. So Sanskrit studies were indeed not banned by the British. The focus of over-all education policy, however, changed in 1835 toward the promotion of English education. Sanskrit as a subject, along with other subjects, continued to be studied in institutions supported by the British, including the universities that opened in 1857 in Bombay, Calcutta and Madras, and later in other cities. Institutions like the Pune Sanskrit College, Benares Sanskrit College and the Calcutta Sanskrit College, with transformations of their structure, continued to be supported by the colonial British government, and many graduates of these institutions served as judges and in other capacities during the British rule. Titles like Mahāmahopādhyāya were also given by the British government. So the British approach to Sanskrit needs to be studied in some detail.
