Dear Prof. Paturi,

  

My comments assumed that the mapping of Sanskrit to English letters was approximate, because whatever the transliteration scheme the Sanskit sounds do not correspond to the English sounds. The usage of f in transliteration was also an approximation - perhaps I should shift to a diacritic based writing. Some of the old Devanagari texts and some grantha texts use a different symbol (but the same one) for upadhmAnIya and jihvAmUlIya. The pronunciation of the upadhmAnIya is known from the shikShA texts as you correctly pointed out.

  

However, I may point one key difference between the upadhmAnIya and the letter f. The labio-dental character of the letter f is clearly seen when it is followed by a vowel, but not so much when it is followed by a consonant, feel vs. free. In the Sanskrit, the upadhmAnIya cannot be followed by a vowel by default, and the taittirIya prAtishAkhyam entails insertion of an extra pa-kaara after the upadhmAnIya. The upadhmAnIya is also not said by itself, so it is half a mAtra andso the difference between the bi-labial vs. the labio-dental character is very minute. But yes, there is no f sound in the Sanskrit, the upadhmAnIya gets an 'f'-like sound.

  

Ramakrishnan
