** "Are not the words 'pustakam, granthah,etc', and the depiction of Saraswati as pustakapaani, etc. evidences of the practice of writing/printing/embossing, etc. for the earliest times? The Bhagavatam has Rukmini writing a letter to Krishna. Of course the Bhagavatam could be argued to be of a"**

** Pustakam is a loan word. It rather is an evidence of the lateness of the written book. Grantha is arranged material that is possible on the oral level. One has to give evidence of writing before Asoka. **

** I should state here that on this count, *ie* on preAsokan post-Harappan writing we are not on absolutely lost ground. There is some similarity of Asokan Brahmi with the Phoenician alphabet of the eighth century BC. But while Brahmi might have got inspiration from the Phoenician, it was of an independent character – partly phonetic and partly syllabic. An admixture of Harappan and Phoenician cannot be ruled out. It will take a lot to demonstrate that – one should consult A.H.Dani’S *Indian paleography*.**
