As you know the *nati sūtra* as formulated in Rgveda Prātiśākhya (5.40-5.61) describes the morpho-prosodic conditioning of **Retroflex Spreading**in nasals n \> ṇ (न \> ण). What I found interesting is that the retroflexion in laterals ḷ (ल \> ळ)in South Indian languages also follows a similar pattern of **retroflex harmony**, and therefore we can apply the rules of *nati* for explaining retroflex lateral (ळ) in Sanskrit words:

  

ऋकाररेफषकारा नकारं समानपदेऽवगृह्य नमन्ति  
अन्तःपदस्थमककारपूर्वा अपि संध्याः संध्य ऊष्माप्यनिङ्ग्ये 5.40

...

एषा नतिर्दन्त्यमूर्धन्यभावः 5.61

  

In Telugu, here are some examples of ळ after र: अरळ, केरळ, खुरळि, गरळ, तरळ, धारळ, परळुलु, प्रळयमु, मुरळि, विरळमु, विरळि, सरळ

  

Here are some examples of ळ after ष: प्रक्षाळन, क्ष्वेळित, क्षाळित etc.

  

I wrote about it in my article in Telugu about four years ago:

  

<http://eemaata.com/em/issues/201203/1933.html?allinonepage=1>

  

However, the reason forळ in plurals such as ūḷḷu, trāḷḷu, kāḷḷu etc. has nothing to do with *nati sūtra*. It is because the original plural in Dravidian has a retroflex -ḷ (It is *-kaḷ* in South Dravidian languages such as Tamil, Kannada etc.). Early (pre-Nannayya) Telugu inscriptions also show -ḷ for non-human plurals.
