Thanks Dr. Narayanan for the excellent clarification. ए and the ओ in Prākṛta indeed have two versions – a short one and a long one. This is different from Saṁskṛta where their is no short version. I think the short ए and the ओ also exist in the Southern Indian languages. The catch in Prākṛta is, unlike the Southern scripts, there is no difference in writing which makes it very difficult for readers to make out the less common short versions unless they are familiar with the prosodic rules of the meter and/or singing the metre.  
  
Many Braja and Avadhī works are full of the short ए and the ओ. The Rāmacaritamānasa of Gosvāmī Tulasīdāsa liberally uses them. There are many examples, but an interesting one is  
  
तब रामहि बिलोकि बैदेही। सभय हृदयँ बिनवति **जे**हि **ते**ही॥ RCM 1.257.4 ॥  
  
where the जे in जेहि is a short syllable, but the ते in तेही is a long syllable so that सभय हृदयँ बिनवति जेहि तेही has 16 instants as required. A half-caupāī of 16 instants generally has the high-level parsing of 6 + 4 + 4 + 2 (for other variations one may refer works like Kāvyaprabhākara or Chandaḥprabhākara of Babu Jagannath Prasad or other Prakrit/Hindi prosody books). Taking either जे or ते as short would make the parsing as 6 + 4 + 4 + 2 but since the first half ends with a spondee (देही of बैदेही), the second half must also end with spondee and so the conclusion is that Gosvāmījī meant the short ए in जे of जेहि.  
  
Those who have learned the singing of the Mānasa from a Guru naturally parse the metre correctly and know which syllable is to be sung long or short.  
Otherwise, due to the lack of indication of short versions of ए and the ओ in the printed versions of the Rāmacaritamānasa, this fine distinction can only be made by those who know the rhythm of the metre - e.g. a half dohā of 24 instants (e.g. तुलसी अलखहि का लखै रामनाम जपु नीच) has the high-level parsing of 6-4-3 6-4-1 or a quarter-harigītikā of 28 instants (e.g. श्रीरामचन्द्र कृपालु भजु मन हरण भवभय दारुणम्) has the fine parsing of 2-2-1-2 2-2-1-2 2-2-1-2 2-2-1-2.  
  
I have not come across a complete prosodic map of the Rāmacaritamānasa so far, but such a work would greatly help new readers of the epic.
