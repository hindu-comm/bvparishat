Dear Dr.Kannan,

Thanks for the references which are all modern and in English. Almost everything that is to be known about the history of zero has been researched and written about by BB Datta in the last century. He was a mathematician,He published not only in the Calcutta mathematical society but also in the American mathematical monthly. His reprinted book "Hindu Mathematics"is still a great reference work. He also quotes Subandhu's Vasavadatta for a simile that uses 'zero'. But he does not refer to Panini. Please share the paper 'zero in Panini" that you have mentioned.. I was looking for discussion on zero (Shunya or Kha or Bindu in that sense) in Sanskrit texts. That it has been used since at least 2000 years is well attested, that is not a mystery. But my curiosity was to know why Naiyyayikas who knew about it did not treat it as Purvapakshä for their analysis. AFAIK they were realists and hence should have formed their opinion about whether this is a real 'sankhya' or not. Dr.Shastry has referred to words such as "nihsés.a". Yes such words indicate the knowledge of 'zero' . Subtraction of two numbers is used in Lagadha's Vedanga Jyotisha. Hence, they should have come across subtraction of a number by itself. But there is no reference to how they handled it. In the Krishi-Parashara, the reminder obtained by the division of the Sáka era year-number by seven is prescribed for prognostication of rainfall. The result is taken as one of the integers 1,2,3,4,5,6,7. So no-reminder (nihsésha) concept was still not widely prevalent in the beginning of S'aka era. It is routinely natural now to take the reminder as 0,1,2,3,4,5,6. I understand from my discussions with you that there is a modern group of young Taarkikas (young-tarks!) who are reviving the tradition. It would be great if you can introduce to them the need for elucidating the epistemology and ontology of zero in traditional style in Sanskrit, (शून्यवाद ) because precise language matters in such an effort.

regards

RNI
