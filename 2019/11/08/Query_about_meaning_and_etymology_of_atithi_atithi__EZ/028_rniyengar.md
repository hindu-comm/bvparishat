Shankar ji there are several lexicons, like the one you kindly pointed through DCS, that list 'titha'' in the sense of **time**. But I am looking for an attested use in any Shaastra or technical text. The Rāmāyaṇa and the Kirātārjunīya use the word ''bahutitha" not the stand alone ''titha" independently.

बहुपूगगणसंघस्य तिथुक् ॥5-2-52\|\| to the best of my understanding (*corrections welcome*) explains how words bahutitha, saṅgatitha, gaṇatitha get formed. The 'titha' here is an unspecific counting index (something like: *that*-*many-eth*) at the end of which something happens. In fact in MBh काले बहुतिथे occurs several times:

अथ काले बहुतिथे पूर्णे प्राप्तो भुजङ्गमः। 12-359-1

अथ काले बहुतिथे वने महति दारुणे। 3-65-2

क्षुधया पीड्यमानस्तु नलो बहुतिथेऽहनि। 3-61-12

तं दृष्ट्वा रक्षिणस्तत्र तथा बहुतिथेऽहनि। 1-107-2

ततः काले बहुतिथे व्यतिक्रान्ते कदाचन। 3-296-1

ततः काले बहुतिथे व्यतीते भरतर्षभ। 3-107-11

ततः पुनः स भगवान्काले बहुतिथे गते। 3-115-38

ततो बहुतिथे काले लोपामुद्रां विशाम्पते। 3-97-13

ततो बहुतिथे काले सुप्तामुत्सृज्य मां क्वचित्॥ 3-65-6

अनश्नाना बहुतिथं शरीरमुदशोषयत्॥ 5-191-22

Always it refers to Kāla or some measure like many days in बहुतिथेऽहनि. The exception is बहुतिथं that refers to शरीर.

But from the context here also ‘long duration’ is meant. It is possible due to such constant usage *tithaḥ* got detached as a separate *samjñā* for time. But how the lexicographers collected the word - from grammar or from antecedent texts?

  

RN Iyengar
