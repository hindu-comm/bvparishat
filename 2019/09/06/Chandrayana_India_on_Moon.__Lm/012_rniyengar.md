Yes. I have this book which Dr.Kannan also shared with me. The author, nearly my namesake (or in reverse!) was a pioneer writing in the 19th cent. He was a self made Indologist, when traditional Sanskrit scholars out of curiosity or zeal to know more learnt English and a few like our author correspondedwith Max Mueller. His introduction is as interesting as the main text. I have cited his work in my publication: Astronomy in Vedic Texts (Book Chapter,*pp107-169**History of Indian Astronomy: A* *Handbook,;*Ed.K.Ramasubramanian *et al,* Publ. IIT Bombay & TIFR, Mumbai) This is what I have written:

  

*It has to be pointed out here that the not so well known Indian scholar Aiyangar,in his writings on Indo-Aryan mythology discussed the TA hymn on*

*Śiśumāra as representing a constellation in the sky and hesitatingly proposedthat the Dhruva of this hymn was perhaps the pole star. He was more interested*

*in gathering and deriving philosophical information from the Vedic andPurāṇa stories. He did not recognize the constellation Śiśumāra in astronomical*

*terms but drifted widely to other astral myths in an effort to match theVedic and the Purāṇic stories without appreciating the effect of precession as*

*recorded in the astral legends. Ref: Aiyangār M. N. Essays on Indo-Āryan Mythology, Bangalore 1898. Available at:(<https://archive.org/details/MN40106ucmf_3>)*

  

There are many efforts by various scholars to bring out celestial connections with some of the RV suktas. I have a fairly bulky collection of such literature. What I have found is that the three types of meanings have been passed on to us through several filters and hence closely interwoven. But when Vedic texts count and state specific numbers (they are in plenty) there is more than what meets the eye. This calls for further research;**vyAkhyAna to bring out the 'intention'**, not just another interpretation.

  

Thanks

RNI
