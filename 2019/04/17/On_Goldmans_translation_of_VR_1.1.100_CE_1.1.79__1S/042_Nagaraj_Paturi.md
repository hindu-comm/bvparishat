7\. There is no great or novel point being made out by Steiner  

that in the*varNa*-scheme prevalent in ancient times, and from times immemorial,  

the*s'Udra*was at one end of the spectrum.

-- Prof. Kannan

  

--- That end can be the right end on the horizontally placed items of the spectrum and need not necessarily the bottom extreme in the vertically , particularly hierarchically placed items of the spectrum.

----------------------

1\. varNa was prevalent in the texts in India in ancient times but not in the society. What was prevalent in the society was caste system. There are hundreds if not thousands of castes in reality. The four Varnas do not fit into these hundreds if not thousands of castes in reality. In the varNa system which is textual Vais'yas are described as agriculturists and traders. We talk of Indian society being predominantly agrarian and 80% of the population living on agriculture , there are truely a huge number of castes with agriculture as their caste-occupation (hereditary occupation). None of these castes is described as Vais'ya. There are a huge number of castes which have trading as their caste-occupation and only one in each region of India is treated as Vais'ya and the other castes though they have trading as caste-occupation. Kshatriyas are referred to as kings and warriors in the texts but the kings in reality most often hailed from different castes such as agricultural castes and other castes and some times even forest tribes that are not treated as Kshatriyas. Inside villages and in most urban settlements, agricultural landlords were/are the most powerful people *a la* Kshatriyas of the texts. But they are not referred to as Kshatriyas. From a text-based point of view they may be described as S'udras but they are not engaged in the paricharyaatmaka activities. That is why they get surprised at their being called as S'udras and the description of S'udras as paricharyaatmaka castes.

  

So it is a textualist flaw to talk about the actual hierarchy among the actual social groups such as castes in Indian society based on the discussions about the textual social groups called varNas.

  

2\. Whether it is in the case of textual social groups called varNas or the actual social groups in reality called castes, stratification or hierarchy is not the defining feature of that grouping.

In the case of varNa , the oft-quoted 'source' of hierarchy among varNas , purusha sukta is actually communicating the opposite of that, i.e., the organic mutually dependent roles of the four varNas.

In the case of actual social groups called castes too , defining feature of castes is they being just endogamous social groups with the caste-occupations as the right cum duty of each of them or the caste-occupations being 100% 'reserved' as a right to each of them and not their stratification or a vertical hierarchical placement or ranking.

  

3\. This does not mean that social stratification or stratification /hierarchicy among social groups is not a fact. Social stratification or hierarchical view of social groups is almost a cultural universal. It is a reflection of the values of the society and value-hierarchy of the society.

  

4\. Contemporary social order in Indian society is a complex mixture of 1.mostly non-hereditory occupations of modernity with occasional cases of hereditary occupations 2. new horizontal and vertical mobilities of castes 3. Government groupings of castes into OC, OBC, SC and ST. 4. effective stratification being among non-hereditory occupations of modernity

  

To show , interpret or misinterpret textual descriptions to misrepresent the reality of the actual social order in India has become predominant trend among the academicians of a certain ideological orientation dealing with Indian society.
