> > Bronkhorst simply ignores those of the other side’s arguments which > he, I suppose, cannot refute. Secondly, he does not ask himself which > arguments are objective (not needing anything other than the literal > meaning of an ancient author’s remark — free from a modern > researcher’s ‘padding’,) and are not contradicted by some other > equally objective piece of evidence and hence weightier. He is also > unmindful of the principle that a view supported by one or more > *mutually independent* pieces of evidence is stronger — that there is > some such thing as cumulative weight of evidence in historical > research. The cumulation may not always lead us to a single definite > or precise conclusion, but it does put us on a path toward such a > conclusion.
> > 
> > 
> >   
> > 
> > 
> > Thus, if the tradition rightly thinks of Jaimini as a junior > contemporary of Bādarāyaṇa and if Jaimini is placed in the B.C. part > of Indian history as most modern scholars seem to have > done,Bādarāyaṇa too must be a B.C. period author. We may not be able > to say exactly where he is to be placed -- how much older than 'the > second or third century B.C. Jaimini' he is, but we can be certain > that he is much older than the third century A.D. where several modern > Vedānta researchers tend to put him (without visiting primary > evidence).
