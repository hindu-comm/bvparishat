Dear Sanhitaji,

  

I do not intend to add fuel to fire, ruffle feathers or promulgate a partisan view as to which method is more commendable, however you may find Otto von Böhtlingk's comments in his Introduction to his translation of Aṣṭādhyāyī interesting. I cannot say for sure what degree of Sanskrit scholarship Otto von Böhtlingk himself held or what authority he enjoys in the modern academic world today (perhaps Dr Madhav Deshpande can comment better) but I personally find his comments quite insightful and interesting, however I am keenly conscious that some of my friends may find them too strong or far-stretched, nonetheless I reproduce them verbatim here.

  

We know that Otto von Böhtlingk (May 30, 1815 – April 1, 1904) was a German Indologist and his first great work was an edition of the Aṣṭādhyāyī, with a German commentary, under the title Acht Bücher grammatischer Regeln (Bonn, 1839–1840).

  

Otto von Böhtlingk expresses his views on Aṣṭādhyāyī and its Prakriya recasts like the Kaumudi in the "*Einleitung*" (i.e. Introduction), p.xvi, 3rd para, of his German translation of the Aṣṭādhyāyī titled "***Panini's Grammatik***" in these words:

*  
*

**Original German:**

*Die Anordnung der Sutra kann uns Her und da befremden, ist aber streng durchdacht und in bewunderungswurdiger Weise durchgeführt. Erstrebt wird die möglichste Kürze und Vermei-dung aller Wiederholungen, und dieses wird ohne alien Zweifel erreicht. Je aufmerksamer man Panini's Grammatik studirt, um so mehr wird man über den Scharfsinn und die glückliche Be-wältigung des ungeheuren Stoffes erstaunen. Sie ist in ihrer Art ein Meisterstück ersten Ranges.Spätere Grammatiken, die Panini's Sutra aus der unverrückbaren Ordnung gebracht haben, um alles dem Stoffe nach Zusammengehörige aneinander zu reihen, sind ohne ausführliche Commentare, die stets auf etwas weit Vorangegangenes oder Folgendes Rücksicht nehmen müssen, ganz unverständlich und als Missgriffe zu betrachten.*

  

**English Translation:**

"The arrangement of the sutras in the Ashtadhyayi appears to be strangely scattered here and there, but actually it is well thought-out and realized in a marvelous way. The purpose is to achieve the most possible brevity and avoid all repetitions, and this is achieved without any doubt. The more attentively one studies Panini's Grammar the more one is amazed by it's very keen insight and one can happily master this enormous subject matter. The Grammar itself is a masterpiece of a superior quality. The later grammars destroyed the unbreakable sequence of Panini's sutras, with the goal of putting together everything that is closely associated, in one place. These grammars are fully unintelligible without extensive commentaries, which must refer to the far foregoing sutras or following sutras, to make any sense at all, so they (the later grammars) must be considered as mistakes."

  

Hope this helps.

  

Regards,
