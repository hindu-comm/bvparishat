> The most thorough review ever published of the kinds of > phenomenological experiences that accompany meditative techniques > appeared in PLoS One this week. It doesn’t focus on just one kind of > meditation but the hundreds practiced around the world. >
> 
> > This paper, out of a cross-disciplinary group from Brown University > and Brown University Medical School, polled Western Buddhists on the > meditative techniques they used and distinguished what they identify > as 59 different classes of psychological reactions to meditation.
> > 
> > 
> >   
> > 
> > Unusual among papers in the field, this article covers in depth > negative as well as positive effects from different methods. Very few > papers have discussed that issue at length. >
> 
> > 
> >   
> > 
> > 
> > Due to ongoing revolutions in systems biology, we know a great deal > today about the neurobiological foundations of different types of > focused attention, or meditation. We also know a lot about the kinds > of physiological changes and alterations in genetic expression that > take place when those techniques are practiced. Data on specific > methods covered in papers like this one can be used along with our new > biological understanding of meditation to generate a complete systems > biology model of meditative techniques — something that doesn’t yet > exist in the field.
