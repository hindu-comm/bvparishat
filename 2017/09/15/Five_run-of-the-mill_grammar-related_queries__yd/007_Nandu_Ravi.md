> > > > Similarly it is श्रीगुरुभ्यो नमः, SrIguru being the word. Whenever > > SrI is appended we are doing a compound श्रीमान् च असौ गुरुः। > > Writing them separately is indeed seen but that shoukd be understood > > as a way of writing and no more. There could be other peculiarities > > in writing. For instance many old manuscripts start with > > श्रीगणेशायनमः without space between the first two words. Whatever be > > the writing convention, while reading we do not observe gap between > > Sri and gaNapati. > > > > 
> > > > 
> > > >   
> > > > 
> > > > 
> > > > The same extends to writing in Roman, including names. For instance > > Indira Priyadarshini Gandhi ought to be Indirapriyadarshini (and > > Nitish Kumar, Nitishkumar) but we observe the gap. This needs to be > > seen as a writing convention and not an exact representation of the > > spoken word. It must be borne in mind that language is always what > > is spoken. Writing is only a representation of the spoken word, but > > not language itself. My approach had been to be careful - at least > > while writing in Devanagari - myself, but to look at forms with gaps > > as conventional, and not "wrong". For instance, introducing gaps in > > the long samaasas (the ones with abhangaSlesha) of Sanskrit prose > > works is actually very useful. > > > > 
> > > > 
> > > >   
> > > > 
> > > > 
> > > > झभञ् does not undergo any change. The marker, ञ् has to be > > pronounced like "n" in "lunch" or पञ्च. This is often pronounced / > > heard as य्ँ but that is not correct. There is a perceptible > > difference in pronunciation of ऋ, ञ, (and conjuncys like ज्ञ), श, ष, > > and स in different parts of India today. Further there are regional > > variations: va \> ba in the East, la \> La in South-central, ta \> > > la in Kerala, ya \> ja in certain parts, ra/la and ra/Da in certain > > parts and so on and further sub-regional variations. For example, in > > Guntur of AP there is a tendency to replace "dhya" with "jja" > > (sanjja, majja etc.). However Sikshaa is clear about the > > pronunciation of each of these and the regional or sub-regional > > variations do not change the correct forms. > > > >
